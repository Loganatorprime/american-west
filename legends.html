Legends of the West

Heroes, Outlaws, and Icons Who Shaped the Frontier

Notorious Outlaws

Jesse James

1847 - 1882

"The Robin Hood of the West"

Leader of the James-Younger Gang, Jesse James became America's most famous outlaw. He robbed banks, trains, and stagecoaches across the Midwest. Killed by Robert Ford for a $10,000 reward.

Outlaw

Billy the Kid

1859 - 1881

Born Henry McCarty, aka William H. Bonney

Legendary outlaw of the Lincoln County War. Said to have killed 21 men, one for each year of his life. Shot and killed by Sheriff Pat Garrett at age 21.

Outlaw

Butch Cassidy

1866 - 1908

Born Robert LeRoy Parker

Leader of the Wild Bunch, the most successful train-robbing gang in history. Known for his charm and non-violent approach. Fled to South America with the Sundance Kid.

Outlaw

John Wesley Hardin

1853 - 1895

"The Deadliest Gunfighter"

Claimed to have killed over 40 men. A Texas gunfighter who started killing at age 15. Studied law in prison and became an attorney before being shot in El Paso.

Outlaw
"There is no law west of St. Louis, and west of Dodge City there is no God."
- Old Western Saying

Legendary Lawmen

Wyatt Earp

1848 - 1929

"The Lion of Tombstone"

Frontier lawman famous for the Gunfight at the O.K. Corral. Served as deputy marshal in Dodge City and Tombstone. Survived the old West to die peacefully in Los Angeles at 80.

Lawman

Wild Bill Hickok

1837 - 1876

Born James Butler Hickok

Legendary gunfighter, lawman, and showman. Served as marshal in Abilene, Kansas. Shot in the back while playing poker in Deadwood, holding what became known as the "Dead Man's Hand."

Lawman

Pat Garrett

1850 - 1908

"The Man Who Killed Billy the Kid"

Lincoln County Sheriff who tracked down and killed Billy the Kid. Former buffalo hunter and bartender who became the most famous lawman in New Mexico Territory.

Lawman

Bass Reeves

1838 - 1910

"The Invincible Marshal"

Born into slavery, became one of the first Black deputy U.S. marshals west of the Mississippi. Arrested over 3,000 felons and was never wounded in 35 years of service.

Lawman

Native American Warriors

Sitting Bull

c. 1831 - 1890

Tatanka-Iyotanka, Hunkpapa Lakota

Holy man and leader who united the Lakota tribes against U.S. expansion. Led the resistance that defeated Custer at Little Bighorn. Later performed in Buffalo Bill's Wild West show.

Warrior Chief

Crazy Horse

c. 1840 - 1877

Tashunka Witko, Oglala Lakota

Legendary war leader who helped defeat Custer at Little Bighorn. Known for his bravery and mystical visions. Never photographed, never signed a treaty, never lived on a reservation.

Warrior Chief

Geronimo

1829 - 1909

Goyaale, Chiricahua Apache

Apache leader who fought against Mexico and the United States for decades. His resistance became legendary. The last Native American leader to formally surrender to U.S. forces in 1886.

Warrior Chief

Chief Joseph

1840 - 1904

Hin-mah-too-yah-lat-kekt, Nez Perce

Led his people on a 1,170-mile fighting retreat toward Canada. Famous for his surrender speech: "I will fight no more forever." Became an eloquent advocate for Native rights.

Warrior Chief
"I will fight no more forever."
- Chief Joseph, 1877

Pioneers & Icons

Calamity Jane

1852 - 1903

Born Martha Jane Canary

Frontierswoman known for her sharpshooting, rough manner, and alleged romance with Wild Bill Hickok. Worked as a scout, ox-team driver, and performer. A true original of the Old West.

Pioneer

Buffalo Bill Cody

1846 - 1917

William Frederick Cody

Scout, bison hunter, and showman. Created Buffalo Bill's Wild West show, which toured for 30 years and shaped the world's image of the American frontier. Employed Sitting Bull and Annie Oakley.

Showman

Annie Oakley

1860 - 1926

"Little Sure Shot"

The most famous sharpshooter in the world. Star of Buffalo Bill's Wild West for 17 years. Could split a playing card edge-on and shoot dimes tossed in the air. A symbol of women's empowerment.

Sharpshooter

Doc Holliday

1851 - 1887

John Henry Holliday, D.D.S.

Dentist turned gambler and gunfighter. Best friend of Wyatt Earp and fought alongside him at the O.K. Corral. Suffered from tuberculosis and moved West seeking a cure. Died in a Colorado sanatorium.

Gunfighter
